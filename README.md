# nelliefinal
North Carolina Labor Market Trends Final Project

This final project analyzes North Carolina labor market trends by examining how unemployment, employment levels, wages, and economic shocks connect over time.

Primary Purpose-The primary purpose of this project is to use data analysis to turn labor market data into business insights that help explain workforce trends, wage pressure, and economic resilience in North Carolina.

 Project Overview

For this project, we studied labor market data for North Carolina using data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics and other supporting sources. The main focus was to understand how unemployment, employment levels, and average hourly wages changed over time.

The project also looks at how major economic disruptions, especially the COVID-19 period, affected the labor market. By using charts, Tableau visuals, and regression analysis, we were able to identify patterns and explain what they mean from a business perspective.

Research Questions

This project focuses on three main research questions:

1. How has the unemployment rate in North Carolina changed over time, and what overall trends can be observed in the labor market?

2. What relationship exists between employment levels and average hourly wages in North Carolina?

3. Are changes in unemployment connected to noticeable changes in employment levels and wages, especially during periods of economic disruption or recovery?

 Data & Method

The main dataset used in this project comes from labor market data related to North Carolina. The data includes variables such as:

- Year/month
- Unemployment rate
- Employment level
- Average hourly wages

The data was cleaned and organized before being used for analysis. We used Excel and Tableau to create charts and visualizations. Regression analysis was also used to test the relationship between employment levels and wages.

The main steps in the analysis were:

1. Collect labor market data
2. Clean and organize the dataset
3. Create visualizations in Tableau and Excel
4. Analyze relationships between unemployment, employment, and wages
5. Interpret the results and connect them to business insights

Visualizations & Final Findings

The project includes visualizations that show:

- Employment versus unemployment trends
- Unemployment rate versus average hourly wages
- Employment level versus average hourly wages
- Wage growth over time
- The impact of the 2020 COVID-19 shock on the labor market

The visualizations helped show that employment and unemployment often move in opposite directions. The data also showed that wages generally increased over time, even during periods when the labor market was disrupted.

Key Findings

Finding 1: Unemployment and wages are related, but not perfectly

The analysis shows a negative relationship between unemployment and wages. This means that when unemployment is lower, wages tend to be higher. However, the relationship is not very strong, which suggests that other factors also influence wage growth.

Finding 2: Employment levels help explain wage growth

The regression analysis showed a positive relationship between employment levels and average hourly wages. As employment increased, wages also tended to increase. This suggests that employment is an important factor in explaining wage growth.

Finding 3: Economic shocks created noticeable labor market changes

The COVID-19 period caused a sharp disruption in the labor market. Employment dropped and unemployment increased during 2020. After that period, employment began to recover, unemployment decreased, and wages continued to rise.

Business Case Report

This project is important because labor market trends affect businesses, workers, and decision-makers. Employers need to understand wage pressure when employment levels rise. Workforce planners also need to look at multiple labor market indicators instead of focusing on only one number.

The analysis can help businesses make better decisions about hiring, wages, and long-term workforce planning.

Recommendations

Based on the analysis, businesses and workforce planners should:

- Track employment and unemployment together instead of looking at only one indicator
- Expect wage pressure when employment levels are high
- Prepare for labor market disruptions during economic shocks
- Use data visualization and regression analysis to support business decisions
- Continue monitoring wage growth because wages may keep rising even during uncertain economic periods

Overall, this project shows that North Carolina’s labor market has changed over time through periods of stability, disruption, and recovery. Employment, unemployment, and wages are connected, but no single factor explains the entire labor market. The strongest insight from the project is that employment levels are closely related to wage growth, while economic shocks such as COVID-19 can create major short-term changes.
